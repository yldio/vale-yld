# Writing Style Guidelines

These rules reduce patterns commonly associated with AI-generated text.

## Vocabulary Restrictions

### Banned Words

Do not use these overused terms:

- `delve` → use: explore, examine, look into, investigate
- `tapestry` → use: mix, combination, variety, collection
- `testament` → use: proof, evidence, example, sign
- `multifaceted` → use: complex, varied, diverse
- `vibrant` → use: lively, active, energetic
- `pivotal` → use: important, key, critical, major
- `realm` → use: area, field, domain, space
- `meticulous` → use: careful, thorough, detailed
- `underscore` → use: show, highlight, demonstrate
- `intricacies` → use: details, complexities, specifics
- `nuanced` → use: subtle, complex, detailed
- `landscape` (figurative) → use: field, area, situation, environment
- `cutting-edge` → use: new, modern, advanced, latest
- `revolutionary` → use: significant, major, important, new
- `embark` → use: start, begin, launch
- `foster` → use: encourage, support, promote
- `leverage` → use: use, apply, take advantage of
- `robust` → use: strong, solid, reliable
- `seamless` → use: smooth, easy, simple
- `synergy` → use: cooperation, collaboration, combination

### Banned Phrases

Never use these constructions:

```
- "It's important to note"
- "It is worth mentioning"
- "No discussion would be complete without"
- "In today's ever-evolving world"
- "In the realm of"
- "A testament to"
- "Stands as a testament"
- "Plays a vital role"
- "Rich cultural heritage"
- "Breathtaking views"
- "Stunning natural beauty"
- "Dynamic hub"
- "Captivates visitors and residents alike"
- "One of the most important things to consider"
- "At its core"
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "From X to Y" (when used for rhetorical effect with vague terms)
```

## Structural Rules

### Sentence Endings

**Do not** end sentences with present participle phrases that claim vague significance:

```
BAD:  "The policy was enacted in 2020, highlighting its importance to the administration."
GOOD: "The policy was enacted in 2020."

BAD:  "She won three awards, underscoring her influence in the field."
GOOD: "She won three awards."

BAD:  "The building was renovated, reflecting the city's commitment to preservation."
GOOD: "The building was renovated as part of the city's preservation program."
```

These phrases (`highlighting`, `underscoring`, `emphasizing`, `reflecting`, `demonstrating`) add false analytical weight. Facts cannot "highlight" things—only people can.

### Transitions

Minimize these overused connectors:

- `Moreover` → prefer: Also, And
- `Furthermore` → prefer: Also, In addition
- `Additionally` → prefer: Also
- `However` → use sparingly, or restructure sentence
- `On the other hand` → use sparingly
- `In addition` → prefer: Also

Let ideas connect naturally. Not every paragraph needs an explicit transition word.

### Summaries and Conclusions

**Never use** section-ending summary phrases:

```
- "In summary"
- "In conclusion"
- "In essence"
- "Overall"
- "To summarize"
- "To sum up"
- "All in all"
```

End sections by finishing the thought. Do not restate what was just said.

### Negative Parallelism

Avoid excessive use of contrast structures:

```
BAD:  "It's not about the destination—it's about the journey."
BAD:  "This isn't just a tool, it's a revolution."
BAD:  "The goal isn't to X, it's to Y."
```

Use these sparingly if at all.

## Formatting Rules

### Em Dashes

Use em dashes (`—`) sparingly. Prefer:

- Commas for minor pauses
- Parentheses for asides
- Colons for introductions

```
BAD:  "The team — which included three engineers — finished early."
GOOD: "The team, which included three engineers, finished early."
GOOD: "The team (which included three engineers) finished early."
```

Do not use multiple em dashes in a single paragraph.

### Lists

Avoid the bolded-heading-plus-explanation pattern:

```
BAD:
- **Performance:** The system runs faster than competitors.
- **Reliability:** Uptime exceeds 99.9%.
- **Cost:** Pricing starts at $10/month.

GOOD:
The system runs faster than competitors, maintains uptime above 99.9%, and starts at $10/month.

ALSO GOOD (when lists are appropriate):
- Runs faster than competitors
- 99.9% uptime
- Starts at $10/month
```

### Bold Text

Use bold sparingly and intentionally. Do not bold:

- Product names repeatedly
- Every key term in a paragraph
- Items in a predictable pattern

### Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length. Do not write paragraphs where every sentence is approximately the same length.

```
BAD:
The project launched in January. The team consisted of five engineers. The budget was set at fifty thousand dollars. The timeline extended through the end of March.

GOOD:
The project launched in January with five engineers and a $50,000 budget. It ran through March.
```

## Content Rules

### Significance Claims

Do not assert vague importance. Show, don't tell:

```
BAD:  "This was a pivotal moment in the company's history."
GOOD: "Revenue doubled within six months of this decision."

BAD:  "The framework plays a vital role in modern development."
GOOD: "Most major tech companies use this framework."
```

### Specificity

Include concrete details. Avoid generic descriptions:

```
BAD:  "The city offers something for everyone, from casual visitors to die-hard enthusiasts."
GOOD: "The city has 47 museums, 12 professional sports teams, and hosts 200+ annual festivals."
```

### Marketing Language

Write informatively, not promotionally:

```
BAD:  "Experience the breathtaking beauty of our stunning oceanfront location."
GOOD: "The building is on the oceanfront."

BAD:  "Our cutting-edge solution revolutionizes the way teams collaborate."
GOOD: "The tool lets teams edit documents together in real time."
```

## Forbidden Patterns

### Never Include

- Knowledge cutoff disclaimers ("As of my last update...")
- Sycophantic openers ("Great question!", "Certainly!", "Absolutely!")
- Meta-commentary ("I'd be happy to help with that")
- Hedging phrases that add no information ("It's worth noting that...")
- Rhetorical questions used as transitions

### Attribution Errors

Do not attribute actions to inanimate objects that only people can perform:

```
BAD:  "This statistic highlights the need for reform."
GOOD: "Researchers cite this statistic as evidence for reform."

BAD:  "The report emphasizes cost savings."
GOOD: "The report shows potential cost savings of 30%."
```

## Self-Check Checklist

Before finalizing any written content, verify:

- [ ] No banned vocabulary words
- [ ] No banned phrases
- [ ] No trailing "-ing" significance clauses
- [ ] No summary/conclusion phrases
- [ ] Varied sentence lengths
- [ ] Specific details included (numbers, names, dates)
- [ ] No marketing-speak
- [ ] Em dashes used sparingly (max 1 per paragraph)
- [ ] Bold used sparingly and intentionally
- [ ] No sycophantic or meta phrases
